Multi-Agent Simulation of Adaptive Behavioral Responses to Infectious Disease Transmission Patterns

Real-Time Influenza (ILI) Forecasting and Commuter-Coupled District Transmission Modeling for Seoul's 25 Districts

By Seungjin Lee
Department of Epidemiology and Health Informatics
under the supervision of Professor Won Jin Lee

ABSTRACT
Seasonal influenza in the post-COVID-19 era has entered an immunity-debt regime - off-season circulation, amplified and earlier peaks, and a distributional shift that silently degrades forecasting models trained on pre-pandemic data. This matters for public health: city-aggregate forecasts mask the district-level hotspots where vaccines, antivirals, and surge capacity are actually allocated, and statistical models that ignore behavior cannot anticipate the behavior-driven rebound that defines this regime. This thesis develops a district-resolution decision-support system for Seoul influenza-like illness (ILI) integrating (i) a multi-model probabilistic forecasting registry, (ii) a behavior-coupled agent-based metapopulation simulation, and (iii) a retrieval-grounded large-language-model (LLM) advisory layer - designed to be spatially targeted, mechanistically interpretable, and operationally actionable.
Across Seoul's 25 districts (gu) with mobility, meteorological, and administrative covariates, 53 forecasting models are benchmarked under a leakage-controlled train/validation/test protocol with a champion selected by the weighted interval score (WIS). The forecast drives a 25-gu commuter-coupled SEIR-V-D metapopulation whose force of infection is modulated by a four-parameter behavioral layer (risk perception, fatigue, compliance threshold, and contact reduction) and an in-run dynamic agent-allocation scheme. The epidemiological value of each component is explicit: the registry quantifies predictability; the behavioral agent model supplies the causal mechanism and - uniquely - counterfactual intervention experiments (age- and occupation-targeted vaccination) that forecasting alone cannot provide; the ARIA layer (agent-MCP-Hermes) renders both into auditable, human-in-the-loop guidance.
On a 68-week test slab, the count-regression champion NegBinGLM-V7 achieved WIS 3.25 and R2 0.928 - roughly a fivefold lower WIS than the classical time-series baselines evaluated on the same slab (WIS 15-19), and it is further benchmarked against cross-verified published influenza-forecasting standards. Crucially, beyond what forecasting shows, the behavioral metapopulation reproduced a real Seoul ILI wave (R2 0.884, 95% prediction-interval coverage 1.00) while the behavior-off variant over-shot the peak - evidence that behavior-disease coupling is necessary for post-COVID realism - and the dynamic-allocation scheme matched a fixed-resolution reference (attack-rate deviation < 0.01) with exact population conservation. Cross-region pattern-similarity analysis and a full WIS / R2 / RMSE / MAE / AUC-ROC / C-index battery, including honest negative findings, bound the claims.
To our knowledge, few influenza systems integrate probabilistic forecasting, behavior-coupled district-scale metapopulation simulation, and an audited LLM advisory layer in one reproducible pipeline for the post-COVID regime. This integration, the demonstrated behavioral mechanism and the regimes in which it is decisive (characterized by global sensitivity analysis and ablations), and the spatially targeted counterfactuals are the central contributions. All formulas, model details, AI-layer specifications, and training-dynamics analyses are provided as supplementary material.
Keywords: influenza forecasting, metapopulation SEIR-V-D, behavioral agent-based model, weighted interval score, post-COVID, public-health decision support

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The non-pharmaceutical interventions deployed against COVID-19 between 2020 and 2022 suppressed seasonal influenza to historic lows across the world, interrupting the antigenic drift and the population-level immune turnover that normally accompany annual epidemics. When restrictions eased, influenza did not simply resume its former rhythm: the 2022 through 2024 seasons in the Republic of Korea bore the signature of an immunity-debt regime [1], with an enlarged susceptible pool, out-of-season circulation, and earlier, sharper peaks that no longer matched the pre-pandemic record. The same pattern was reported internationally and is now understood as a durable, not transient, change in influenza dynamics that public-health forecasting must accommodate rather than treat as an anomaly.
For surveillance-driven forecasting this is a distributional shift in the strict statistical sense: the joint distribution of incidence and its drivers in the evaluation period differs from that of the training period, so a model fitted to pre-2020 dynamics silently loses calibration precisely when accurate guidance is most needed. The cautionary history of data-driven influenza tracking, most visibly the over-fitting failure of early big-data surveillance [2], shows that statistical skill achieved in one regime need not survive into another and that leakage-controlled, probabilistically scored evaluation is essential rather than optional [3]. Seasonal forcing through temperature and humidity [4] and the demonstrated coupling between human mobility and transmission [5] further imply that a credible model must represent the drivers of change, not merely extrapolate the curve.
Seoul concentrates roughly a fifth of the national population across twenty-five administrative districts (gu) whose commuting intensity, population density, and age structure differ markedly. Operational influenza preparedness, namely the placement of vaccine, antiviral stock, and hospital surge capacity, is decided at this district scale, yet most published forecasting targets a single city- or nation-aggregate series that averages away exactly the spatial heterogeneity that metapopulation theory identifies as decisive for where epidemics travel, synchronize, and peak [6, 7]. A citywide point forecast can be accurate in aggregate and still be silent about the district where the next surge will land.
A second limitation is mechanistic. Purely statistical forecasters describe what incidence will do but not why, and in particular they cannot represent the behavioral feedback - heightened risk perception, adherence fatigue, and voluntary contact reduction - that shapes the post-COVID rebound and that behavioral-epidemic theory places at the center of modern transmission dynamics [8, 9]. This matters operationally because the questions public health actually asks are counterfactual: how much would a given contact reduction avert, and how should a fixed vaccine budget be allocated across age and occupation groups [10, 11]? Such questions are meaningless for a curve-fitter and answerable only by a mechanistic model that carries susceptibility, vaccination, and behavior explicitly.
These gaps motivate an integrated rather than a single-method approach. This thesis asks whether a reproducible system that (i) selects a best probabilistic forecaster from a broad model registry, (ii) couples that forecast to a district-resolution behavioral agent-based metapopulation model, and (iii) exposes both through an auditable language-model advisory layer can deliver influenza guidance that is simultaneously accurate, mechanistically interpretable, spatially targeted, and operationally usable for the post-COVID regime. The emphasis throughout is on honest, leakage-controlled evaluation and on reporting failure modes as openly as successes.
The contributions are fourfold. First, a leakage-controlled benchmark of fifty-three forecasting models on Seoul influenza-like illness identifies a count-regression champion that decisively outperforms the classical time-series baselines that dominate applied practice. Second, a twenty-five-district commuter-coupled SEIR-V-D metapopulation augmented with a four-parameter behavioral layer reproduces a real Seoul wave that a behavior-free variant over-shoots, evidencing that behavior-disease coupling is necessary, not cosmetic, for post-COVID realism, and enabling age- and occupation-targeted counterfactual interventions that forecasting alone cannot provide. Third, an in-run dynamic agent-allocation scheme concentrates simulation resolution where prevalence is highest while conserving the represented population exactly and remaining statistically unbiased. Fourth, the entire pipeline is rendered into auditable, human-in-the-loop guidance and reported with explicit honest negatives, so that its limits are as legible as its strengths.

CHAPTER 2. RELATED WORKS
Short-term influenza forecasting matured through sustained collaborative evaluation. The U.S. CDC FluSight initiative standardized probabilistic targets and a common scoring rule, and successive multi-model assessments established two robust regularities: ensembles tend to outperform their component models, and probabilistic skill must be judged with proper scores rather than point error [12, 13]. The weighted interval score adopted as the FluSight primary metric is a quantile-based, proper approximation of the continuous ranked probability score [3, 14], and a relative weighted interval score below one against a strong reference is the field's working definition of skill [13]. Methodologically, the literature spans mechanistic transmission models, statistical and Bayesian time-series models [15], and machine-learning approaches including non-mechanistic empirical-Bayes forecasters [16]; classical mechanistic data assimilation also produces calibrated seasonal forecasts [17]. Digital and big-data surveillance broadened the input space [18] but also produced its most cited failure, the over-fitting of an early flu-tracking system that looked skillful in-sample and then degraded out-of-sample [2]. The post-COVID resurgence sharpened all of these concerns: pandemic suppression followed by an immunity-debt rebound [1] created out-of-distribution dynamics that degraded pre-pandemic-trained models. The Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency sentinel ILI system supplies the weekly signal used here, and its known reporting-lag and denominator caveats are exactly the kind of surveillance-count subtlety that statistically principled count models are designed to handle [19].
Mechanistic epidemic modeling rests on the compartmental formulation of Kermack and McKendrick, still the backbone of the field [20, 21]. Metapopulation extensions couple subpopulations through mobility and reproduce the spatial invasion, synchrony, and heterogeneity observed at city and district scales [6, 7]. Where individual heterogeneity and contact structure matter, agent-based models on realistic social networks capture dynamics that mean-field compartments cannot, and they have been used to design pandemic-mitigation and source-containment strategies [22, 10, 11]. Transmissibility itself is not constant: it is modulated by seasonal forcing through temperature and absolute humidity [4] and by human mobility, whose collapse and staged recovery during COVID-19 provided a natural experiment on the behavioral lever of transmission [5]. Estimating the time-varying reproduction number from such dynamics is now standard practice and underpins situational awareness [23, 24]. This thesis builds on the metapopulation tradition but instantiates it at gu resolution for Seoul and drives it with a data-selected forecast rather than fixed parameters.
Behavior is the missing endogenous term in most operational models. Risk perception, adherence fatigue, and social learning feed back onto contact rates, and behavioral-epidemic theory formalizes this coupling, from awareness-spreading dynamics that can halt outbreaks [9] to game-theoretic accounts of voluntary vaccination and protective behavior [25]. Two reviews map the design space and its evidentiary gaps: a broad survey of how human behavior has been incorporated into transmission models [8] and a systematic review of behavioral-change models that documents both their promise and their frequent lack of empirical calibration [26]. Empirically, infection-prevention behavior has measurable effects on transmission that models routinely omit [27]. The post-COVID rebound is, in this light, a behavioral phenomenon as much as an immunological one, and reproducing it requires a model in which contact responds to perceived risk. The behavioral agent layer developed here is a concrete, calibrated instance of this coupling at district scale, with an explicit behavior-off control that isolates its causal contribution.
On the methodological frontier, gradient-boosted decision trees remain strong tabular baselines for structured forecasting problems [28, 29], random forests provide stable importance estimates and a basis for parsimonious feature selection [30], and deep sequence models together with time-series foundation models such as Chronos and a decoder-only general forecaster have recently been applied to epidemic and general forecasting [31, 32]. Their advantage over well-tuned simpler models on short, noisy, regime-shifted public-health series is, however, not guaranteed, a tension this work evaluates directly. Honest uncertainty quantification has likewise advanced: distribution-free conformal prediction and its adaptive variants supply finite-sample marginal coverage without distributional assumptions and are well suited to shifting regimes [33, 34]. In parallel, large language models have been explored as agents that retrieve evidence, call tools, and draft analyses; for public-health use their value rests on grounding, faithfulness, and auditability rather than fluency, motivating retrieval-augmented, tool-using designs with explicit provenance. The present work treats this language-model layer as an interpretation and governance surface, not a forecasting engine, and confines its technical detail to the supplementary material.
Evaluation in this thesis follows established probabilistic-forecasting practice and adds an operational, decision-oriented framing. Probabilistic accuracy is measured by the weighted interval score and complemented by prediction-interval coverage and probability-integral-transform calibration [3, 14]; point accuracy by RMSE, MAE, and R-squared; and, because the operational task is to flag weeks above the epidemic threshold, discrimination by AUC-ROC and concordance between predicted risk ordering and realized outbreak timing by Harrell's C-index [35]. Pairwise forecast superiority is tested for significance with the Diebold-Mariano test rather than asserted from point estimates [36]. In sum, the literature offers strong forecasting and strong mechanistic modeling largely in isolation, with behavior coupling, district resolution, and an audited decision layer rarely combined and seldom evaluated together under a single leakage-controlled protocol. This thesis targets that intersection for post-COVID Seoul influenza, and benchmarks its accuracy against the cross-verified published standards above rather than in isolation.

CHAPTER 3. METHOD
3.1 Data and notation

Figure 1. System overview: from KDCA surveillance data through preprocessing, the 53-model registry, the WIS champion, the 25-gu SEIR-V-D metapopulation with a behavioral agent layer, to the audited ARIA advisory layer.
The primary signal is the weekly Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency sentinel influenza-like-illness (ILI) rate for Seoul, expressed per 1,000 outpatient visits, spanning the 2019 through 2025 surveillance seasons. Let y_t denote the ILI rate in week t for the city aggregate and y_{t,g} the rate for district g in 1..25. Sentinel ILI is a count-derived surveillance signal subject to reporting lag, revision, and denominator change, properties that motivate count-based statistical treatment rather than a Gaussian approximation [19].
Covariates X_t comprise three blocks. Calendar and seasonality terms encode harmonic sine and cosine at 52-, 26-, and 13-week periods together with a season index, capturing the winter-dominant cycle and its post-COVID distortion. Meteorological fields from the Korea Meteorological Administration - temperature, relative humidity, precipitation, wind, and short-range forecast values - encode the seasonal forcing that modulates transmissibility [4]. Administrative mobility and population measures - commuter flow between districts, subway and bus boarding density, hotspot and resident population - encode the contact substrate whose variation tracks transmission [5]. Autoregressive lags of the target supply short-memory persistence. All series are held in a single SQLite database of roughly eighty million rows across seventy-nine tables and read read-only for analysis; the eight-stage preprocessing chain that standardizes, transforms, and de-leaks these inputs is documented in the supplementary material.
3.2 Forecasting model registry and representative models
Fifty-three models are organized into families spanning the methodological landscape so that the comparison is broad rather than favorable to a preferred method. The families are: gradient-boosted and bagged trees (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, RandomForest) [28, 29, 30]; regularized and count-regression linear models (ElasticNet, BayesianRidge, the negative-binomial generalized linear model, and a Poisson autoregression); kernel methods (kernel ridge regression, support-vector regression); classical time series (ARIMA, SARIMA, SARIMAX, Theta) and a seasonal naive baseline; extended surveillance and renewal models (EpiEstim and Wallinga-Teunis reproduction-number models [23, 24], count time-series and surveillance models [19], and early-aberration detectors); deep tabular and modern time-series networks (feed-forward variants, PatchTST, iTransformer, Mamba, TimesNet, N-BEATS, N-HiTS, TiDE, TCN); conformalized quantile regressors [33]; graph networks; a time-series foundation model [31]; and ensembles. The complete registry with per-model specifications is catalogued in the supplementary material; the body presents only the formulations needed to interpret the results.
Three representative formulations anchor the families that produced the champion and the baselines. The negative-binomial generalized linear model, which provides the study champion, represents the ILI count Y with mean mu = exp(x' b) and variance mu + mu^2 / phi, so the dispersion parameter phi absorbs the over-dispersion typical of surveillance counts; it is well suited to non-negative, over-dispersed, low-to-moderate counts and yields interpretable multiplicative covariate effects. The Poisson autoregressive model links the current rate to recent lags through a log link, log mu_t = a + sum_k rho_k log(y_{t-k} + 1), capturing short-memory persistence with a likelihood appropriate to counts. The seasonal ARIMA family models the differenced series with autoregressive and moving-average terms at weekly and seasonal lags; it is a strong descriptive baseline, but its linear-Gaussian assumptions fit over-dispersed ILI counts poorly during a regime shift, a mismatch the results below make concrete. Tree ensembles and deep networks are included as flexible nonlinear comparators whose tendency to overfit a short record is precisely what the leakage-controlled protocol is designed to expose.
3.3 Metapopulation SEIR-V-D with a behavioral agent layer
Each of the twenty-five districts maintains compartments susceptible (S), exposed (E), infectious (I), recovered (R), vaccinated (V), and deceased (D), coupled through a commuter mobility matrix M whose entry M_gh is the fraction of district-g residents present in district h on a given day [6, 7]. The compartmental skeleton follows the standard formulation [20, 21], extended with vaccination and disease-death compartments so that the model can answer intervention questions. With district population N_g and force of infection lambda_g, the deterministic dynamics are:
    dS_g/dt = - lambda_g S_g - nu_g S_g + omega R_g
    dE_g/dt =   lambda_g S_g - sigma E_g
    dI_g/dt =   sigma E_g - gamma I_g - delta I_g
    dR_g/dt =   gamma I_g - omega R_g
    dV_g/dt =   nu_g S_g ,   dD_g/dt = delta I_g ,
    lambda_g = beta(t) * c_g(t) * sum_h M_gh * I_h / N_h .
In plain terms, susceptible residents become exposed at a rate set by how much infection they meet where they actually spend the day, become infectious after a short latent period, and then recover, are vaccinated out of susceptibility, or die. The parameters carry direct epidemiological meaning: beta(t) is baseline transmissibility with optional seasonal forcing [4]; sigma is the rate of progression to infectiousness, its inverse the latent period of about two days; gamma is the recovery rate, its inverse the infectious period of about three and a half days; delta is the small influenza fatality rate; nu_g is the district vaccination rate; and omega is the slow waning that returns recovered individuals to susceptibility across seasons. The basic reproduction number is approximately beta over gamma, and the time-varying reproduction number is monitored as an epidemic-validity check [23].
The compartmental rates are fixed to literature-consistent values and then refined against Seoul data rather than asserted. The latent period sigma^-1 of about two days follows systematic-review estimates for influenza, the infectious period gamma^-1 of about three to four days follows viral-shedding studies, and the case-fatality term delta is small and seasonal. Rather than accept textbook constants wholesale, the transmissibility and seeding are calibrated by particle-filter maximum-likelihood (a few hundred particles) against the weekly Seoul series, which yields a basic reproduction number near 1.4 with a tight confidence interval - within the inter-quartile range reported for seasonal influenza. The vaccination rate nu_g is set from district vaccine-coverage records, and the waning rate omega is calibrated so that recovered individuals return to susceptibility over a multi-season horizon, which is what allows the model to represent the post-COVID immunity-debt build-up rather than a single isolated wave.
The distinctive term is the behavioral contact multiplier c_g(t) in [0,1], generated by a four-parameter agent layer rather than fixed by hand, operationalizing the behavior-disease coupling that behavioral-epidemic theory identifies as central [8, 9]. Each agent carries a risk perception that rises with local prevalence and decays over an approximately thirty-day memory (dR/dt = alpha * I_g/N_g - lambda_R R), a fatigue state that accumulates while complying and relaxes with time constant tau, and a compliance decision taken when risk net of fatigue exceeds a personal threshold theta; compliant agents cut their contacts, lowering c_g(t) and therefore transmission. Allowing the threshold to vary across agents lets population-level compliance emerge from heterogeneous individual decisions rather than being imposed, in the spirit of agent-based and game-theoretic behavioral models [22, 25, 26]. Setting alpha = 0 and theta to infinity yields a behavior-off control that recovers a standard mean-field metapopulation, isolating the causal contribution of behavior.
To keep district-resolution agent simulation tractable, an in-run dynamic-allocation scheme adjusts the number of simulated agents between epochs in proportion to prevalence - high resolution at the peak, fewer agents off-season - and rescales the population by a particle-filter systematic resampling that conserves the represented population exactly and is unbiased in expectation. The epidemic trajectory is thereby preserved while computation is concentrated where it is informative; the algorithm, its conservation proof, and its validation against a fixed-resolution reference are given in the supplementary material.
3.4 Uncertainty quantification
Point forecasts are converted into calibrated prediction intervals by split-conformal and adaptive conformal inference applied to each model's prior-fold out-of-fold residuals, which guarantees finite-sample marginal coverage without distributional assumptions and adapts to the distribution shift that defines the post-COVID regime [33, 34]. Quantile and conformalized-quantile models emit predictive intervals directly. These intervals are the input to the weighted interval score, itself a proper scoring rule that rewards both sharpness and calibration [3, 14], and to the coverage and probability-integral-transform diagnostics reported in the results. Because conformal calibration uses only past residuals, it introduces no leakage from the evaluation span.
In the post-COVID regime this distribution-free guarantee matters more than usual: a Gaussian interval calibrated on the suppressed seasons would be far too narrow for the rebound, whereas the conformal quantile widens automatically as recent residuals grow. Intervals are emitted on the standard quantile grid - the central 50-, 80-, and 95-percent levels and the finer ladder the weighted interval score averages over - so the same machinery serves both the coverage diagnostics and the score that selects the champion, and adaptive conformal inference re-estimates the quantile online so that coverage tracks rather than lags the distribution shift.
3.5 Evaluation protocol
Models are fitted and selected under a strict temporal split designed to exclude leakage, following collaborative-forecasting practice [12, 13]. A training and validation span is used for fitting, hyper-parameter search, and out-of-fold model selection; a held-out test span comprising the most recent sixty-eight weeks is used only for final evaluation. A separate, later real span is reserved exclusively as the forward target consumed by the metapopulation simulation and the advisory layer, and is never used to fit or select forecasting models. The champion is chosen on validation by the weighted interval score and then reported on the sealed test span, so that selection and reporting never share data and the winner's-curse bias of selecting on the test set is avoided. A per-fold leakage audit re-computes any future-knowable feature - quantile bins, interaction terms, and thresholds - within each fold, so that no feature is constructed with knowledge of the evaluation period.
Within the training and validation span, model selection uses walk-forward cross-validation rather than ordinary k-fold, because a time series must always be learned from past and tested on future. The window expands fold by fold: each fold trains on all weeks up to a cut-off and is scored on the block immediately after it, so the validation residuals that feed conformal calibration and the one-standard-error rule are themselves genuinely out-of-sample. The most recent sixty-eight weeks are then held out entirely from this loop, and the fourteen-week real span is sealed beyond that, giving the three nested, non-overlapping partitions - train/validation, test, real - on which the leakage-controlled claims rest.
3.6 Large-language-model and ARIA advisory layer
The system exposes its forecasts and simulations through ARIA, a language-model advisory layer used strictly for interpretation and governance, not for forecasting. A general-purpose model orchestrates read-only domain tools - database queries, the champion forecast, reproduction-number estimation, scenario simulation, and literature retrieval - and a tamper-evident audit ledger records every query, tool call, and response so that any guidance can be traced to its evidence. The layer takes no autonomous action and may state only what the tools return; its full architecture, grounding policy, and operational evaluation are given in the supplementary material.
3.7 Evaluation metrics
A full battery of one hundred twenty-four metrics is computed for every model, but the body reports a consistent six-metric panel chosen for public-health interpretability. The weighted interval score measures probabilistic accuracy and is the selection criterion (lower is better), being a proper score that jointly rewards calibration and sharpness [3, 14]. R-squared, RMSE, and MAE measure point accuracy. For the operational task of flagging weeks above the KDCA epidemic threshold, AUC-ROC measures discrimination and Harrell's C-index measures concordance between the predicted risk ordering and realized outbreak timing [35]. The full catalogue across eight metric families, the complete per-model values, and the model registry are provided in the supplementary material; the six-metric panel is a faithful projection of that battery.
Each panel metric answers a distinct operational question, which is why all six are reported together rather than collapsed into one number. The weighted interval score asks whether the whole predictive distribution is sharp and calibrated; R-squared, RMSE, and MAE ask how close the point forecast is - in explained variance and in the original per-1,000 units; AUC-ROC asks whether the model separates epidemic from non-epidemic weeks at any alert threshold; and Harrell's C-index asks whether it orders weeks by risk in the correct sequence, which is precisely what a week-to-week escalation decision consumes. Reporting them side by side prevents a single favourable figure - a high R-squared driven by the peak, for instance - from masking a mistimed onset call that would matter operationally.
3.8 Champion selection
The champion is the model attaining the lowest out-of-fold validation weighted interval score, a single criterion fixed in advance. R-squared, interval coverage, and the remaining panel metrics are reported alongside as diagnostics rather than combined into a composite score, so that a low-WIS model with weak coverage stays visible rather than being masked by aggregation. Pairwise differences between the champion and its competitors are then tested for statistical significance on the held-out span with the Diebold-Mariano test [36], so that the ranking rests on inference rather than on point estimates alone.

The analysis covers weekly Seoul ILI from the 2019 through 2025 surveillance seasons. Across the twenty-five districts the same wave is broadly synchronous, but its amplitude varies by a factor that tracks commuting inflow and residential density, so the busiest gu crest well above the citywide mean while quieter ones stay below it; this is the heterogeneity the district-resolution model is built to exploit and that an aggregate forecast discards.

4.4 Metapopulation SEIR-V-D and multi-agent simulation
Driven by the champion forecast and the commuter mobility matrix, the twenty-five-district SEIR-V-D model produces district-level epidemic curves whose relative timing and amplitude follow the observed synchrony-with-heterogeneity pattern [6, 7], and whose city aggregate passes the epidemic-validity checks on the reproduction number [23], seasonal phase, and compartment conservation. Because the model carries explicit susceptible, vaccinated, and deceased compartments and a behavioral contact term, it supports operations that forecasting cannot: counterfactual placement of a fixed vaccine budget across age and occupation strata, and what-if exploration of contact-reduction policies, evaluated district by district. This is the same class of intervention question that agent-based pandemic-planning models were built to answer [10, 11, 22], here specialized to gu-resolution post-COVID Seoul and anchored to a data-selected forecast rather than to assumed parameters. The multi-agent realization lets the contact decision vary across individuals, so that population-level compliance emerges from heterogeneous personal thresholds rather than being imposed [25], and it ingests the real forward signal so that the simulated present stays aligned with observed surveillance.
Three counterfactual experiments make the operational value concrete. A non-pharmaceutical-intervention scenario scales the baseline transmissibility downward to mild and to lockdown-level contact reduction; a school-closure scenario cuts school-age contacts over a defined window; and a vaccination scenario advances a fixed dose budget into susceptibles before the season, with the option to target the elderly or high-contact occupations first. Each is run against the no-intervention baseline on the same calibrated dynamics, so the averted peak and the shift in onset week are read off as differences rather than asserted. These are precisely the questions a district health office asks - where, when, and on whom to spend a limited vaccine and outreach budget - and they are answerable only because the simulation carries the susceptible, vaccinated, and behavioral compartments explicitly; a forecasting curve, however accurate, cannot represent an intervention that was never in its training data.

4.7 Diebold-Mariano significance tests
Lower error on a single test sample may reflect luck rather than a real difference, so pairwise superiority is assessed with the Diebold-Mariano test, which compares two models' forecast-error series and asks whether their mean loss differs by more than sampling noise would explain - in plain terms, whether one competitor is genuinely better or merely better on these particular weeks [36]. The champion's improvement over the classical time-series baselines is statistically significant by this test. The same procedure compares simulation variants, such as behavior-on against behavior-off, placing the mechanistic claims on the same inferential footing as the forecasting claims rather than resting them on point estimates alone. Where the test cannot separate two strong models on the short span - as between the champion and the runner-up - that indistinguishability is reported rather than overstated.
Operationally the test forms the per-week loss-differential series between two models and rejects equal accuracy when its mean is large relative to an autocorrelation-robust standard error, which is the appropriate correction for serially dependent weekly forecast errors. Applied here, the champion's advantage over each classical time-series baseline is significant, and the behavior-on versus behavior-off contrast in the simulation is likewise separable, so both the forecasting ranking and the mechanistic claim that behavior is necessary rest on inference rather than on a single point estimate. The champion and the runner-up Poisson autoregression cannot be separated on sixty-eight weeks, and that is reported as a tie rather than presented as a decisive win.

4.9 Methodological findings and honest negatives
Several findings cut against the system and are stated directly. The classical time-series family does not merely underperform but fails, with negative R-squared and chance-level discrimination, a reminder that descriptive baselines can be actively misleading under regime shift and echoing the cautionary record of over-confident data-driven surveillance [2]. The champion's raw prediction intervals under-cover at the ninety-five-percent level before conformal recalibration. The system is validated on a single city: the cross-region analysis establishes only that Seoul's seasonal pattern resembles other temperate systems, not that the model forecasts them, so multi-site validation remains future work. The real evaluation span is short, at fourteen weeks, and therefore weak for inferential claims; and the agent simulation's behavioral parameters are calibrated to ILI and to Seoul and would require re-calibration for a different pathogen, a higher-severity response, or another city - the kind of conditional, hard-to-transfer calibration that behavioral-model reviews repeatedly flag [26]. These limits are consolidated here rather than scattered, and none is concealed by the headline metrics.

The forecasts and simulations are exposed through ARIA, a language-model advisory layer organized as three cooperating tiers: a retrieval-and-tool tier that answers only from the project database and computed artifacts; an agent-orchestration tier that selects and sequences those tools under a fixed contract; and an audit tier that records a tamper-evident, hash-chained log of every query, tool call, and response for human review. The layer translates quantitative output into auditable, human-in-the-loop guidance and takes no autonomous action. CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
5.1 What the registry and the data reveal
Two empirical facts organize the discussion. First, across fifty-three models the decisive winners are count-aware regressions, not the elaborate deep or foundation architectures and not the classical time-series baselines that fail outright; on over-dispersed, short, regime-shifted ILI counts, a model whose likelihood matches the data-generating process - the negative-binomial mean-variance relationship - beats both flexible approximators that overfit the brief record [31, 32] and Gaussian models that mis-specify the noise. This is consistent with the collaborative-forecasting lesson that calibration and an appropriate likelihood, not architectural complexity, are what carry skill [12, 13]. Second, the district-resolved data show that a single citywide curve averages away amplitude heterogeneity that is operationally decisive, exactly as metapopulation theory anticipates [6, 7]. Together these motivate the integrated design: a likelihood-appropriate forecaster for accuracy, and a district-resolved mechanistic model for where and why.
Because the purpose of training is prediction, the discussion is anchored on predictive performance shown the way reviewers expect it: observed against predicted over the held-out period, quantified by the error measures conventional in influenza-forecasting studies. The champion tracks the post-COVID rebound closely one step ahead (MAE 4.20, RMSE 6.97, MAPE about 16 percent, SMAPE about 16 percent, R-squared 0.93), and its relative weighted interval score against a seasonal baseline is well below the FluSight skill threshold of one, placing it within the range reported for skillful collaborative forecasters rather than judging it in isolation [3, 13]. Validation is deliberately same-region: because measurement, denominator, and context differ across surveillance systems, performance is reported on Seoul's own held-out forecasts, and the cross-region material is read only as evidence that Seoul's seasonal shape is shared by comparable temperate systems, never as a transfer claim.

5.2 Epidemiological value of the integrated approach
The metapopulation SEIR-V-D, the district resolution, and the multi-agent behavioral layer each add something forecasting alone cannot. The compartmental structure makes susceptible depletion, vaccination, and waning explicit, so the model answers counterfactual questions - how a fixed vaccine budget should be split across age and occupation strata, or how much a given contact reduction would avert - that are meaningless for a curve-fitter and that have historically required agent-based planning models [10, 11, 22]. The commuter coupling propagates infection along the actual geography of daily movement, reproducing the observed synchrony-with-heterogeneity and locating risk where people congregate rather than where they sleep [7]. The behavioral layer closes the loop that defines the post-COVID regime: because contact responds to perceived risk, the model reproduces a real wave that a behavior-free version over-shoots, and the multi-agent realization shows population compliance emerging from heterogeneous thresholds rather than being assumed [9, 25]. The dynamic-allocation result shows that this fidelity is affordable at district scale, which is what makes routine operational use plausible rather than merely demonstrative.
5.3 Validity, bias, and public-health significance
Validity must be argued, not asserted. R-squared, though reported, is an imperfect public-health metric: it rewards tracking the mean and is dominated by the peak, so a model can post a high value yet miscall the onset week that actually triggers action - which is why the weighted interval score, interval coverage, lead time, and outbreak discrimination are reported beside it [3, 14]. Potential confounding of the mobility-ILI association by shared seasonality, holidays, and weather is addressed by adjusting for those covariates and by reading the association through a causal rather than a raw-correlation lens; the strength, consistency, temporality, biological plausibility, and dose-response of the mobility signal are consistent with a Bradford-Hill reading of a contributory rather than spurious relationship [37], and accord with the documented mobility-transmission link [5]. The public-health significance is concrete: district-level probabilistic forecasts with calibrated alerts, a mechanistic substrate for targeted vaccination, and an audited interpretation layer together support earlier, more spatially precise, and more accountable preparedness than any component alone.
5.4 Limitations
The limitations are real and bounded. The behavioral parameters are calibrated to influenza and to Seoul; transfer to another pathogen, a higher-severity response, or another city would require re-calibration. The study is single-site: the cross-region analysis shows only that Seoul's dynamics resemble comparable temperate systems, not multi-site forecast validation, which the wider literature flags as essential before generalization [38, 26]. The real evaluation span is short, so prospective claims are descriptive rather than inferential, and a genuinely prospective, multi-season evaluation in the FluSight mould remains future work [12, 13]. The forecaster's raw intervals under-cover before recalibration [34]. The stochastic agent kernel retains residual integrator sensitivity that makes single-seed point fits more reliable than naive seed ensembles. And the language-model layer, however well audited, remains an interpretation surface whose outputs require human ratification.
5.5 Core contribution
The central claim is narrow but defensible: for post-COVID, district-scale influenza in Seoul, coupling a likelihood-appropriate probabilistic forecaster to a behavior-aware metapopulation simulation, and exposing both through an auditable advisory layer, yields guidance that is accurate, mechanistically interpretable, spatially targeted, and operationally accountable, with its failure modes reported as openly as its successes. The demonstrated necessity of behavioral coupling for post-COVID realism, and the affordability of district-resolution agent simulation through dynamic allocation, are the contributions most likely to transfer beyond this study; the integration of forecasting, behavior-coupled simulation, and an audited decision layer under one leakage-controlled protocol is, to our knowledge, rarely assembled for the post-COVID regime.
5.6 Conclusion
This thesis built and evaluated an integrated, district-resolution influenza decision-support system for post-COVID Seoul. A leakage-controlled benchmark of fifty-three models identified a count-regression champion that decisively outperformed classical time-series baselines on probabilistic accuracy, point error, and outbreak discrimination, and whose accuracy sits within the range of skillful collaborative forecasters when benchmarked against published standards [3, 13]. The champion forecast drove a twenty-five-district behavioral SEIR-V-D metapopulation that reproduced a real Seoul wave only when behavior-disease coupling was active [9, 8], and an in-run dynamic-allocation scheme made district-scale agent simulation affordable without bias. Together with an auditable advisory layer and an explicit account of the system's limits, these results show that likelihood-appropriate forecasting and behavior-aware mechanistic simulation are complementary, and that their combination is a practical path to earlier, spatially targeted, and accountable influenza preparedness. Future work should extend the behavioral layer to severity- and policy-driven responses, lengthen prospective evaluation to multiple seasons, and test transfer across pathogens and cities.

S4. Preprocessing, feature stability, and hyper-parameter optimization
Every model is fitted through one identical wrapper pipeline so that comparisons are fair; the stages and their formulas are given below. All fold-dependent quantities are estimated on the training portion of each fold only.
S4.1 Target transform and scaling
Each model selects a target transform appropriate to its likelihood. Count and linear models use the identity; deep and variance-sensitive models use the log1p map y' = ln(1 + y), inverted with a safety-capped exponential yhat = expm1(clip(z, -2, ln(1 + 10 * y_max_train))) so that a single large prediction in transformed space cannot explode after back-transformation (this cap removes the catastrophic R-squared collapse seen without it). Additional options are square-root, inverse-hyperbolic-sine asinh(y) = ln(y + sqrt(y^2 + 1)), and rank transforms. Features are standardized as z = (x - mu_train) / sigma_train using only training-fold statistics. Prediction sanitization maps only non-finite values (NaN, +/-inf) to zero and preserves all valid magnitudes, including legitimate zeros and negatives, so that downstream interval and score computations are never silently corrupted.
S4.2 Feature stability selection
Feature selection is stability-based and sample-size-adaptive. For small folds, features are screened by the absolute correlation with the target, |corr(x_j, y)|; for large pools, by model-based permutation importance averaged over repeats. The retained set is then trimmed by a one-standard-error parsimony rule: among candidate sets whose out-of-fold weighted interval score is within one standard error of the best, the smallest set is chosen, argmin over k of |S_k| subject to WIS(S_k) <= WIS_best + SE(WIS_best) [30]. A family-aware safeguard keeps deep networks on the stability-anchored subset rather than trusting importances estimated on a small fold. Leakage-prone quantile, interaction, and threshold features are re-coded inside each fold from training-only statistics (S8).
S4.3 Hyper-parameter optimization
Hyper-parameters are tuned per model by Tree-structured Parzen Estimator Bayesian optimization. The objective is the out-of-fold weighted interval score, L(theta) = mean over folds of WIS(y, F_theta), minimized on the same fold splits used for feature selection so that tuning and selection never see the test span. Studies are persisted and warm-started across runs; each trial runs in an isolated subprocess for memory hygiene with per-trial cleanup, and the trial budget adapts to sample size as n_trials = max(10, min(50, round(0.3 * n_train))).
S4.4 Conformal prediction intervals
Point forecasts become calibrated intervals by split-conformal inference on each model's prior-fold residuals. With nonconformity scores s_i = |y_i - yhat_i| on a calibration set of size n_c, the (1 - alpha) prediction interval is yhat +/- q, where q is the k-th smallest score with k = ceil((n_c + 1)(1 - alpha)); adaptive conformal inference updates this quantile online so that coverage tracks the post-COVID distribution shift [33, 34]. These intervals feed the weighted interval score and the coverage diagnostics.
S5. Large-language-model background
The advisory layer uses general-purpose large language models as orchestrators that call domain tools and summarize their outputs; it does not use them as forecasters. Backends include a hosted API model and a local model, chosen at run time. The governing principle is grounding: the model may state only what the tools return from the project database and computed artifacts, and unsupported assertions are treated as failures rather than stylistic lapses.
S6. ARIA architecture
ARIA comprises three tiers. The tool tier exposes read-only database queries (restricted to SELECT-class statements through a SQL guard), a forecast tool that loads the champion artifact and runs inference, and tools for model comparison, reproduction-number estimation [23], outbreak detection, epidemic-validity checks, scenario simulation, and retrieval over a citation index. The orchestration tier is a Model-Context-Protocol server that advertises these tools under a fixed schema and is reachable from a local web dashboard through a bridge. The audit tier (Hermes) writes a SHA-256 hash-chained ledger of every request, tool call, and response, so any guidance can be traced to its evidence and any tampering is detectable.
A statute-and-surveillance knowledge block augments these tiers. Compiled from the Infectious Disease Control and Prevention Act (감염병예방법) and Korea Disease Control and Prevention Agency (KDCA) records and injected into the system prompt of every model and conversation mode, it encodes the legal classification of notifiable diseases—influenza is a Class 4 disease under sentinel surveillance, which is precisely the influenza-like-illness signal forecast in this study—alongside the statutory basis for case reporting, epidemiological investigation, non-pharmaceutical interventions such as gathering restrictions and school closure (Article 49), and immunization (Articles 24–25). The disease grades, surveillance type, and most recent ILI figures are regenerated directly from the project database, so the advisory layer cites actual Korean law and KDCA data rather than fabricating statute numbers or disease classes. These trajectories are also exposed through an interactive twenty-five-district dashboard in which each metapopulation SEIR-V-D intervention scenario is annotated with its statutory basis, and a bidirectional link lets the advisory layer read the current simulation state and highlight districts on the map.
S7. Language-model evaluation
The advisory layer is evaluated on four operational metrics rather than open-ended generation quality: tool-call faithfulness (whether the model calls the correct tool and reports its output without distortion), latency, human-override rate, and hallucination on the retrieval tool (claims unsupported by retrieved sources). A multi-backend comparison runs the same scenario prompts through each backend and scores them on these axes; the prompt set and results accompany this thesis. No clinical or autonomous decision is delegated to the layer, consistent with its role as an interpretation and governance surface rather than a decision maker.
S8. Additional methods
This section collects supporting procedures referenced in the main text: the leakage audit (per-fold re-computation of future-knowable versus revealed features, so that quantile and interaction features never see the future); the implementation environment (a single command-line entry point, a single SQLite database with a read-only analytics overlay, and device-agnostic execution across CPU, CUDA, and Apple Silicon); alert-threshold sensitivity around the KDCA epidemic cutoff; reproduction-number estimation settings [23, 24]; the complete feature-importance rankings; the eight-stage wrapper chain in full; and infrastructure validation.
Stochastic ensembling and global sensitivity. Because the agent layer is a binomial tau-leap process, a single seeded run is one realization rather than the model's behavior; epidemic summaries are therefore reported as the mean and the 2.5th–97.5th percentile interval over a Monte Carlo ensemble of independent seeds, with a running coefficient of variation used to justify the replicate count [39]. Parameter influence is assessed by global, variance-based sensitivity rather than one-at-a-time perturbation: a Latin-Hypercube design varies the disease and behavioral parameters simultaneously over their ranges, and partial rank correlation coefficients (PRCC) with significance quantify each parameter's monotone effect on attack rate, peak timing, and mortality [40]. Transmissibility dominates the attack rate (PRCC ≈ +0.92), while the behavioral risk-sensitivity parameter is a strong negative lever (PRCC ≈ −0.83), confirming that adaptive behavior is influential across the parameter space even where the observed seasonal series is forcing-dominated.
S9. Training dynamics and improvement analysis
Drawn from the run artifacts, this section documents how performance evolved during fitting. Per-model Bayesian-optimization trajectories show the out-of-fold weighted interval score decreasing across trials; the cross-phase metric history tracks each model from baseline through final evaluation; and the champion evolves to the lowest-WIS model. Removing the legacy composite selection gate simplified champion choice to a single best-WIS rule and consolidated the recorded evaluation into the 124-metric battery (138 columns including bootstrap confidence bounds) without loss of information. The in-run dynamic-agent scheme scaled resolution with prevalence - from a floor of a few hundred agents off-season to roughly twelve thousand at the peak - while conserving the represented population and preserving the attack rate to within 0.002 of a fixed-resolution reference. Behavioral calibration improved the real-wave fit to R-squared 0.884. All figures derive from the recorded optimization databases and metric logs.
